# CFD Scheme — Analysis

The following analysis references a report<sup>[1](references#ref1)</sup> containing information about the governments 
own evaluation of the CFD scheme, conducted between 2018 and 2021, commisioned by the 
Departemnt for Business, Energy and Industrial Strategy (BEIS). The evaluation assesed 
the extent to which the scheme was meeting its intended goals and objectives across the 
first three allocation rounds (AR!, AR2 and AR3).

---

The overall conclusion of the report was that the CfD scheme was successful in meeting its intended core 
objectives, which included giving private investors confidence to invest in UK Renewable energy, making that 
investment cheaper to secure, attracting a broader range of investors and keeping costs down for consumers.

The evidence for this conclusion can be seen in several key statistics from the evaluation. One such statistic 
was that the scheme was able to save consumers an estimated £3 billion compared to the previous Renewables Obligation 
scheme across AR1, AR2 and AR3, and when including future projected projects, total savings could reach £10 billion 
by 2050. It was also found that the 15 year price guarantee reduced interest rates for developers' loans by up to 2 
percentage points. Furthermore, AR3 alone was able to award 5.5GW of offshore wind capacity, which is enough to power over 7 
million homes. These figures show that the scheme has been effective in both expanding renewable energy capacity and reducing 
costs for consumers.

However, there were parts in the report that noted the scheme's failure in promoting investment in smaller renewable technologies 
such as marine and bioenergy and even saw investment decline compared to the previous scheme. A similar failure occured in AR5, 
where even offshore wind ran into problems due to the strike price being too low, resulting in zero offshore wind bids for the 
first time since the scheme began.

Overall, the evidence suggests that the CFD scheme has been broadly successful in achieving its aims, however its long term 
effectiveness depends on the government setting strike prices correctly. The grid connection delays and the AR5 failure also 
show that there are still significant challenges to overcome.
