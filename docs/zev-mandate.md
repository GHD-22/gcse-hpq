# The Zero Emission Vehicle (ZEV) Mandate

## 1. Introduction to the Policy

The ZEV Mandate is a legal obligation placed on vehicle manufacturers, requiring them to sell a rising percentage of 
zero emission vehicles each year, with the aim of trying to decarbonise the transport sector. If manufacturers 
don't comply, they face fines of up to £15,000 per car that falls short of the required ZEV amount. It came into 
legal force in 2024 under the Zero Emission Vehicles, Infrastructure and Charging Act. Unlike CFDs, this is a 
regulatory policy, working through laws and rules rather than financial incentives. This policy was necessary 
because of the fact that the transport sector is the single largest source of UK greenhouse gas emissions at 
around 26% of the total, with road transport making up 91% of that. This lies within the UK government's broader 
commitments to reaching net zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050.

---

## 2. How the Mandate Works

The ZEV Mandate applies to vehicle manufacturers and importers who sell cars 
and vans in the UK - companies such as Volkswagen, Ford, Toyota etc. Every 
year, manufacturers must ensure that a minimum percentage of their sales are 
ZEVs, with these targets rising each year.

**Car targets under the ZEV Mandate:**

| Year | Minimum ZEV Sales (%) |
|------|----------------------|
| 2024 | 22% |
| 2025 | 28% |
| 2026 | 33% |
| 2028 | 50% |
| 2030 | 80% |
| 2035 | 100% |

By fining manufacturers £15,000 for each car short of this goal, the mandate 
ensures they comply. However, this doesn't mean manufacturers are rigidly locked in. 
One thing they can do is borrow from future years' allowances. What this means 
is that, for example, if a manufacturer is supposed to sell 22% ZEVs, but only 
manages 18%, if they sell extra ZEVs the next year to make up for the shortfall, 
they can avoid paying the fines for that year, giving them flexibilty if they had 
a particularly bad year due to various reasons, such as supply chain issues. The 
other thing they can do is trade ZEV credits with other manufacturers who are 
exceeding their targets. ZEV credits are awarded per 1 ZEV sold. They have a 
monetary value and are treated and traded like commodities. This trading systems 
is a deliberate feature of the mandate rather than a loophole. If a manufacturer 
is good at selling EVs (e.g. Tesla) and they exceed their targets, they will have 
extra ZEV credits. Smaller manufacturers can then buy those credits from that 
manufacturer. This means that the overall number of ZEVs sold across the industry 
still meets the target, smaller manufacturers get breathing room and larger 
manufacturers like Tesla get financially rewarded for surpassing their targets. 
Another key point about the Mandate is that it is a supply side policy, meaning 
it focuses on what manufacturers produce and sell, not what consumers bu.y

## 4. Who Does It Affect?

1. **Manufacturers**: Large scale manufacturers like Volkswagen, Ford and Toyota 
are the most directly affected with the mandate forcing them to restructure 
their production and sales around rising ZEV targets. This means:
- Investing heavily into EV production lines 
- Shifting marketing efforts towards electric models.

2. **Consumers**: Consumers are affected indirectly a few ways. One is that the 
mandate increases the supply and variety of electric vehicles available to buy. 
However, some argue that without demand side support - that is, support that 
targets the consumers and influences what they want to buy, like subsidies 
from the government to reduce the cost of EVs - consumers might not buy the EVs 
manufacturers are being made to sell. This is because EVs remain significantly 
more expensive than their petrol and diesel equivalents on average.

3. **Charging Infrastructure**: Due  the surge of EVs being sold because of 
the mandate, pressure was created to expand the charging network in the UK. In 
2024, the UK had 60,000 charge points, yet those points were unevenly distributed, 
with very few in rural areas, reducing confidence people have in EVs. Without enough 
charging infrastructure, the mandate is limited in its ability push consumers to buy 
EVs.

---

## 5. Criticisms and Challenges

One criticism of the mandate was that it was forcing manufacturers to sell more EVs each year, despite 
demand for EVs not keeping pace. In 2024, multiple manufacturers including Ford and Stellantis warned 
that the targets set were too aggressive compared to the demand. This creates risks for 
manufacturers that they will sell EVs at a loss or they will have to pay fines instead.

Another issue is that the average price for an electric vehicle remains substantially higher than that 
of a petrol or diesel equivalent. The mandate is designed to decarbonise road transport, yet the consumers 
who drive the most and make up the majority of road users in the UK are the least able to afford the EVs 
being sold on the market as a result of the mandate. This leads to the mandate having a limited impact on 
overall road transport emissions in the short term. 

Furthermore, the mandate only applies to the sales of new vehicles, while the majority of UK drivers buy 
used cars - meaning the impacts could take up to 15 - 20 years to fully materialise even if targets are 
met, since the average age of a car on UK roads is around 8-9 years
