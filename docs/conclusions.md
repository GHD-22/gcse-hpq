# Conclusion

This project has analysed two UK climate change policies, the Contracts for Difference scheme and the Zero Emission Vehicle Mandate, 
evaluating their effectiveness and efficiency in achieving their environmental aims and their economic impact, looking at reports 
and datasets from official sources, both government and public bodies. The data shows that while the ZEV Mandate has been effective 
in increasing the number of BEVs on UK roads, the impact that this has on the environment is very little, due to the fact that only 
a fraction of the UK's entire car fleet are BEVs, meaning it needs other policies, in order to have any real effect. The CFD scheme 
has been broadly effective in attracting private investment in renewable energy and reducing costs for consumers, saving an estimated 
£3 billion compared to the previous scheme. However, there are still many drawbacks with this policy, such as the fact that these 
projects might only start generating electricity and get connected to the national grid up to 10 years after they were awarded the 
contract

Overall, while these policies are showing promising results, they still have many flaws that reduce their long-term effectiveness. 
That said, it is worth noting that these policies are relatively new, meaning that there is still quite some time until 2050 for 
them to be developed further and fine-tuned, so that they can function long-term and, hopefully, bring the UK down to net zero by 2050.
